## 4.7. Computational Complexity Analysis

To theoretically validate the efficiency of our encoder-free design, we analyze the time complexity of the BiDecT architecture compared to standard encoder-decoder models.

For a given video represented by $G$ GOPs, the multimodal feature extraction produces three feature vectors (appearance, semantic, and motion) per GOP. The multimodal feature embedding module then projects and combines them into a unified multimodal sequence of length $V = 3G$. Let $L$ and $L'$ denote the sequence lengths of the forward and backward captions, respectively, and let $d$ be the model dimension.

Since feature extraction is typically performed offline prior to training, we restrict our analysis to the core trainable components. Under this scope, the multimodal feature embedding module contributes only a negligible $\mathcal{O}(V \cdot d)$ cost, as it consists entirely of linear-time operations. Furthermore, caption lengths ($L, L'$) are bounded by a fixed maximum length in video captioning systems, whereas the multimodal sequence length $V = 3G$ scales linearly with video duration.

In standard encoder-decoder architectures for video captioning, each modality is typically processed by a dedicated Transformer encoder. Within each encoder layer, the self-attention mechanism computes pairwise affinities across the $G$ tokens of its respective modality, while the subsequent FFN sub-layer processes each token independently, yielding a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d + G \cdot d^2)$. When multiple such encoders operate in parallel across different modalities, the aggregate encoder cost remains $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d + G \cdot d^2)$ since the number of modalities is a fixed constant. As the number of GOPs increases, this cost grows quadratically and becomes the dominant computational bottleneck.

By contrast, the BiDecT architecture entirely eliminates the intermediate encoding stage, relying exclusively on cross-attention mechanisms within the decoders to query the multimodal sequence. For the Backward Decoder (BD), the computational cost per layer arises from the masked self-attention over the reverse text, the cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, and the FFN sub-layer, yielding a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}({L'}^2 \cdot d + L' \cdot V \cdot d + L' \cdot d^2)$.

Similarly, the Forward Decoder (FD) computes masked self-attention over the forward text, cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, an additional cross-attention to the global backward context $\overleftarrow{H}$ (of length $L'$), and the FFN sub-layer, yielding a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}(L^2 \cdot d + L \cdot V \cdot d + L \cdot L' \cdot d + L \cdot d^2)$.

Since the number of decoder layers is a fixed hyperparameter, the overall complexity of BiDecT is determined by the sum of these two per-layer costs. Critically, our design completely removes the computational cost of the encoder, $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d + G \cdot d^2)$. Instead, the model’s interaction with the multimodal sequence scales linearly with respect to the video length as $\mathcal{O}\big((L+L') \cdot V \cdot d \big)$, while the remaining terms (decoder self-attention, backward context cross-attention, and FFN) are all bounded and independent of the video length, since $L$, $L'$, and $d$ are fixed by design.

In summary, BiDecT reduces the dominant cost associated with video length from quadratic to linear, offering a more scalable architecture for video captioning, particularly as video length increases.
