## 4.7. Computational Complexity Analysis

To theoretically validate the efficiency of our encoder-free design, we analyze the time complexity of the BiDecT architecture compared to standard encoder-decoder models.

For a given video represented by $G$ GOPs, the multimodal feature extraction produces three feature vectors (appearance, semantic, and motion) per GOP. The multimodal feature embedding module then projects and combines them into a unified multimodal sequence of length $V = 3G$. Let $L$ and $L'$ denote the sequence lengths of the forward and backward captions, respectively, and let $d$ be the hidden dimension of the model.

Since feature extraction is typically performed offline prior to training, we restrict our analysis to the core trainable components. Under this scope, the multimodal feature embedding module contributes only a negligible $\mathcal{O}(V \cdot d)$ cost, as it consists entirely of linear-time operations. Furthermore, caption lengths ($L, L'$) are bounded by a fixed maximum length in video captioning systems, whereas the multimodal sequence length $V = 3G$ scales linearly with video duration.

In standard encoder-decoder architectures for video captioning, each feature stream is typically processed by a dedicated Transformer encoder. The self-attention mechanism within each encoder computes pairwise affinities across the $G$ tokens of its respective modality, incurring a per-encoder complexity of $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d)$. When multiple such encoders operate in parallel across different feature streams, the aggregate encoder cost remains $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d)$ since the number of streams is a fixed constant. As the number of GOPs increases, this quadratic cost grows rapidly and becomes the dominant computational bottleneck.

By contrast, the BiDecT architecture entirely eliminates the intermediate encoding stage, relying exclusively on cross-attention mechanisms within the decoders to query the multimodal sequence. For the Backward Decoder (BD), the computational cost per layer arises from the masked self-attention over the reverse text and the cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, yielding a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}({L'}^2 \cdot d + L' \cdot V \cdot d)$.

Similarly, the Forward Decoder (FD) computes masked self-attention over the forward text, cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, and an additional cross-attention to the global backward context $\overleftarrow{H}$ (of length $L'$), yielding a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}(L^2 \cdot d + L \cdot V \cdot d + L \cdot L' \cdot d)$.

The overall complexity of BiDecT is the sum of these two decoder costs. Critically, our design completely bypasses the $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d)$ quadratic bottleneck associated with encoder self-attention. Instead, the model's interaction with the multimodal sequence scales linearly with respect to the video length as $\mathcal{O}\big((L+L') \cdot V \cdot d \big)$. While the FD introduces an extra text-to-text cross-attention term $\mathcal{O}(L \cdot L' \cdot d)$ to process the backward context, this cost remains bounded and independent of the video length, since both $L$ and $L'$ are fixed by design. Note that the FFN sub-layers are present in both encoder-decoder and decoder-only architectures. Since FFN operates independently on each token without introducing any inter-token interactions, its cost does not contribute to the quadratic bottleneck and remains common to both designs; it is therefore omitted from this comparative analysis. In summary, by removing the intermediate encoding stage, BiDecT reduces the dominant video-length-dependent cost from quadratic to linear, offering a more scalable architecture for video captioning, particularly as video length increases.
