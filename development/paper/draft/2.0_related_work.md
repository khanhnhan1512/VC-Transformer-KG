# 2. Related Work

## 2.1. Video Captioning

The fundamental goal of video captioning is to automatically generate natural language descriptions that comprehend the dynamic visual content of videos. Early pioneering approaches heavily relied on template-based methods `<cite>`, which first detected semantic attributes (e.g., objects, actions, scenes) and subsequently mapped them into pre-defined rigid sentence structures. With the advent of deep learning, the paradigm shifted towards sequence-to-sequence learning, predominantly utilizing the CNN-RNN (Encoder-Decoder) framework `<cite>`. In these models, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) extracted visual features, while Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or LSTMs translated these features into sentences.

Recently, inspired by the tremendous success of the attention mechanism in NLP, Transformer-based architectures `<cite>` have dominated the video captioning field. Researchers have explored various methodologies to capture rich spatial-temporal representations. For instance, SwinBERT `<cite>` proposed an end-to-end transformer that operates directly on sparsely sampled video frames. Other works, such as VASTA `<cite>` and ORG-TRL `<cite>`, focused on integrating object relational graphs and spatio-temporal attention to capture fine-grained interactions between entities. Meanwhile, models like TextKG `<cite>` and STOA-VLP `<cite>` incorporated external knowledge graphs and explicit object-action modeling to enrich the semantic representation. Despite achieving state-of-the-art performance, most of these contemporary frameworks rely heavily on complex, computationally expensive intermediate Encoder blocks to fuse multimodal features. Furthermore, their decoding processes are predominantly unidirectional (Left-to-Right), thereby failing to fully exploit the holistic semantic context of the video.

## 2.2. Bidirectional Decoding for Sequence Generation

Conventional Transformer decoders generate text in a strictly autoregressive, Left-to-Right (L2R) manner. While effective, this unidirectional constraint inherently prevents the model from utilizing right-to-left (R2L) future contexts during the generation of the current word. To address this limitation, bidirectional decoding was initially introduced in neural machine translation and speech recognition `<cite>`, proving that anticipating future semantic structures significantly improves sentence fluency and accuracy.

In the domain of video captioning, recent studies have begun to explore bidirectional decoding. Notably, BiTransformer `<cite>` and BTKG `<cite>` augmented the standard Transformer by introducing a backward decoder. In their designs, the backward decoder generates a reverse sequence to capture the R2L context, which is subsequently fed into the forward decoder to guide the final L2R generation. However, bidirectional decoding often suffers from the "information leakage" problem during training, where the forward decoder trivially copies the exact future tokens provided by the backward decoder. To circumvent this, recent works `<cite>` adopted a *pseudo-reverse caption* strategy, which randomly shuffles the reversed ground-truth captions within the same video pool, effectively forcing the model to learn genuine semantic dependencies.

**Our Difference:** In this paper, we adopt the aforementioned pseudo-reverse caption strategy to prevent information leakage. However, diverging significantly from BiTransformer and BTKG—which still rely on stacked, complex Encoder modules to pre-process visual and knowledge features before decoding—our proposed **BiDecT** introduces a novel **encoder-free** architecture. We entirely discard the intermediate encoder block and design a specialized Multimodal Feature Embedding layer that maps diverse features (visual, semantic, and motion) directly into a unified space. These rich multimodal representations are then directly injected into both the Backward and Forward Decoders. This streamlined design minimizes information loss, reduces structural redundancy, and maximizes the bidirectional guidance for generating highly accurate captions.
