In this section, we conduct extensive ablation experiments to gain a deeper insight into the impact of various architectural components and hyper-parameters on the overall performance of our proposed model. 

Considering the three datasets utilized in our main experiments, MSVD is relatively small in scale, comprising fewer than 2000 videos. Consequently, ablation results on this dataset might be prone to statistical bias and may not accurately reflect general trends. Conversely, VATEX is a massive dataset with over 41000 video clips. While it is excellent for benchmarking final performance, conducting numerous repetitive ablation trials on VATEX would incur prohibitively high computational costs and time consumption. 

Therefore, MSR-VTT emerges as the most optimal choice for these analytical studies. With exactly 10000 videos covering 20 diverse categories, MSR-VTT strikes a perfect balance: it provides a sufficiently large and comprehensive data distribution to ensure that the empirical results are reliable and generalizable, while keeping the overall training time manageable. 

Therefore, we analyze the benchmark MSR-VTT dataset from different aspects to better understand our approach.

## 6.1. Effect of different input information

To evaluate the effectiveness of different input information in our method, we conduct several ablation experiments on the MSR-VTT dataset. The detailed results are summarized in `Table-???`.

**Single Feature Evaluation.** To investigate the individual contributions of the visual ($F_V$), semantic ($F_S$), and motion ($F_M$) features, we first experiment with using only a single modality as the input. As observed, the visual feature yields the best performance (61.79 CIDEr), followed closely by the semantic feature (60.32 CIDEr), while the motion feature contributes the least to the overall accuracy (51.02 CIDEr). The relatively lower performance of the motion feature can be attributed to the limited capacity of the MViTv2-small model used for its extraction, which might not capture complex spatio-temporal dynamics as comprehensively as larger networks. Conversely, the superior performance of both the visual and semantic features strongly highlights the crucial role of the powerful vision-language foundational model, BLIP-2, as it is heavily involved in extracting both of these high-quality representations.

**Two-Feature Combinations.** Next, we evaluate the combinations of any two features, incorporating the feature-type embedding layer. We observe that pairing the visual feature with any other modality consistently leads to a noticeable performance improvement over using a single feature alone (63.00 CIDEr and 62.21 CIDEr). However, combining only the semantic and motion features (without the presence of the visual feature) results in significantly inferior performance (61.17 CIDEr). This indicates that spatial visual information remains the most indispensable foundation for generating accurate captions.

**The Role of Feature-Type Embedding.** Furthermore, we investigate the necessity of the feature-type embedding layer. When fusing all three features without this embedding layer, the model's performance deteriorates drastically (61.18 CIDEr). This severe drop underscores the critical importance of the feature-type embedding layer in our architecture; without it, the model struggles to distinguish the distinct origins and modality characteristics of the input sequence, leading to severe semantic confusion within the shared representation space.

**Full Input Configuration.** Finally, when all three features are jointly integrated and explicitly distinguished by the feature-type embedding layer, our proposed model reaches its optimal capacity (64.29 CIDEr). This complete configuration achieves the highest scores across all four evaluation metrics, proving that visual, semantic, and motion representations are highly complementary when properly embedded.

## 6.2. Effect of GOP Numbers

We investigate the impact of the number of sampled GOPs on the model's overall performance and efficiency. Theoretically, extracting a larger number of GOPs provides the model with a more fine-grained and detailed temporal view of the video, but it simultaneously incurs a higher computational cost during both training and inference. 

To find the optimal balance, we analyze the distribution of GOP counts within the MSR-VTT training set and select five representative values: 9, 10, 13, 16, and 20 GOPs. These values perfectly correspond to the 25th, 50th, 75th, 85th, and 95th percentiles of the dataset's GOP distribution, respectively. As illustrated in `Table-???`, when the number of GOPs increases from 9 to 13, the CIDEr score exhibits a clear upward trend, peaking at 64.29. However, when the number of GOPs exceeds this threshold (i.e., 16 and 20 GOPs), the performance saturates and even slightly degrades, potentially due to the introduction of redundant visual information. 

Meanwhile, the total inference time on the test set strictly increases as more GOPs are processed, which perfectly aligns with our theoretical expectation. These empirical findings solidly justify our decision to adopt the 75th percentile as the standard sampling threshold across all three datasets in our main experiments (as detailed in `Section-???`), as it yields the most optimal trade-off between captioning accuracy and computational efficiency.

## 6.3. Effect of the Number of Stacked Decoder Layers

The depth of the Transformer network is a critical hyper-parameter that dictates the model's learning capacity. In this ablation study, we systematically vary the number of stacked Transformer layers for both the Backward Decoder (Dec_BD) and Forward Decoder (Dec_FD) from 1 to 6 layers. 

`Table-???` presents the evaluation metrics alongside the total number of trainable parameters for each configuration. As observed, no single configuration strictly dominates across all four evaluation metrics. For instance, the 4-layer configuration achieves the highest BLEU@4 score (48.92), while the 2-layer configuration obtains the best METEOR score (31.50). However, as we heavily prioritize the consensus-based CIDEr metric for its superior alignment with human judgment, the 3-layer configuration emerges as the absolute best choice. Setting both Dec_BD and Dec_FD to 3 layers achieves the highest CIDEr score of 64.29 while maintaining a moderate model size of 53.1M parameters.

## 6.4. Effect of the Number of Heads in Attention Module

In the Multi-Head Attention mechanism, the number of heads determines how many representation subspaces the model can simultaneously attend to. To find the optimal configuration, we vary the number of attention heads from 1 to 128 while keeping the overall hidden dimension constant. The results are reported in `Table-???`.

Similar to the observations made regarding the number of stacked decoder layers, no single head configuration strictly dominates across all four evaluation metrics. For instance, setting the number of heads to 8 yields the highest METEOR score (31.53), whereas using 64 heads marginally achieves the best ROUGE-L score (65.53). Notably, we observe a distinct downward trend in the CIDEr score as the number of heads aggressively increases from 4 to 128 (dropping from 64.29 to 62.40). This degradation suggests that over-segmenting the hidden dimension into too many subspace heads might dilute the learned feature representations, making it difficult for the model to capture coherent multimodal alignments. Since we prioritize the CIDEr metric for comprehensively evaluating the captioning performance, setting the number of attention heads to 4 emerges as the optimal choice.

## 6.5. Effect of the Hyper-parameter $\lambda$

In our proposed training objective, we introduce the hyper-parameter $\lambda$ to balance the loss preferences between the forward decoder and the backward decoder. Theoretically, the value of $\lambda$ should fall within the range of $[0.5, 1.0]$ to ensure that the forward sequence generation (which is the ultimate goal of the task) remains the primary focus. We conduct experiments by varying $\lambda$ from 0.5 to 1.0 with a step size of 0.1, as detailed in `Table-???`.

The most critical observation occurs at the extreme case where $\lambda = 1.0$. Under this setting, the backward decoder does not participate in the optimization process, effectively reducing our model to a conventional unidirectional decoder. Consequently, the performance drops drastically, yielding the worst results across all metrics (e.g., a minimum CIDEr score of 61.45). 

Conversely, when the backward decoder is involved in the training process ($0.5 \le \lambda < 1.0$), all four metrics exhibit substantial improvements compared to the $\lambda = 1.0$ baseline. This strongly validates our hypothesis that the right-to-left semantic context effectively guides the shared multimodal embeddings and enhances sentence structure. Specifically, when we set $\lambda = 0.6$, a stable and optimal balance is achieved between the two decoders. At this configuration, the proposed model obtains the best performance on three out of the four metrics, peaking at 48.54 for BLEU@4, 31.30 for METEOR, and 64.29 for CIDEr.
