# Section: 5. Experiments

## 5.1. Experimental settings

### 5.1.1. Datasets

To evaluate the effectiveness and generalization capability of our proposed BiDecT model, we conduct experiments on three widely used video captioning benchmarks: MSVD, MSR-VTT, and VATEX.

**MSVD** `<cite>` comprises 1970 video clips, each paired with approximately 40 candidate captions. On average, these clips have a duration of about 10 seconds. Following the standard split adopted by prior work, we allocate 1200 samples for training, 100 for validation, and the remaining 670 for testing.

**MSR-VTT** `<cite>` is a generic, large-scale video captioning dataset containing 10000 videos from 20 diverse categories. Each clip is described by 20 human-annotated captions. Consistent with the conventional split, we divide the data into 6513 samples for training, 497 for validation, and 2990 for testing.

**VATEX** `<cite>` is a massive dataset comprising approximately 41269 video clips, where each clip is manually annotated with 10 English and 10 Chinese captions. In this work, we solely utilize English captions. The official split allocates 25991 videos to the training set, 3000 to the validation set, and 6000 to the public test set. We exclude the private test set (containing about 6278 videos) due to the lack of accessible ground-truth captions. Since a portion of the original YouTube videos is no longer available, we utilize the hosted data provided by the Common Visual Data Foundation and Yang et al. `<cite-MultiCap>`. Consequently, our final split consists of 25985 training videos, 3000 validation videos, and 5808 testing videos.

### 5.1.2. Evaluation Metrics

To comprehensively and objectively evaluate the quality of the generated captions, we employ four standard metrics **computed via the Microsoft COCO Evaluation Server** `<cite>`: BLEU@4 (B4) `<cite>`, METEOR (M) `<cite>`, ROUGE-L (R) `<cite>`, and CIDEr (C) `<cite>`.

These metrics provide complementary perspectives on caption quality. Specifically, **BLEU@4** measures n-gram precision to assess sentence fluency. **METEOR** evaluates semantic alignment by considering exact matches, stems, and synonyms. **ROUGE-L** focuses on structural similarity based on the longest common subsequences.

Most importantly, we prioritize CIDEr, a metric specifically designed for captioning tasks, as it captures the consensus between generated captions and human references to reflect the accuracy of key information.

### 5.1.3. Implementation Details

**Video Pre-processing.** Prior to feature extraction, we standardize all raw videos across the MSVD, MSR-VTT, and VATEX datasets. Specifically, each video is resized to 240 pixels on its shortest edge and compressed using the H.264 codec via FFmpeg. Following the configuration provided by CoCap `<cite>`, we set the `KeyInt` parameter to 40, meaning each Group of Pictures (GOP) contains exactly 1 I-frame followed by up to 39 P-frames or B-frames. To balance feature granularity and computational efficiency, we analyze the distribution of GOP counts in the training set of each dataset and adopt the 75th percentile as our sampling threshold. Consequently, we uniformly sample a maximum of 9, 13, and 9 GOPs per video for MSVD, MSR-VTT, and VATEX, respectively.

**Multimodal Feature Extraction.** We perform a consistent feature extraction process across all sampled GOPs to obtain visual, semantic, and motion representations:
*   **Visual and Semantic Features:** For each GOP, we strictly focus on the I-frame as the primary source of spatial information. We employ the **BLIP2-OPT-2.7b** model from the Hugging Face Transformers `<cite>` library. First, we extract the $\text{[CLS]}$ token generated by the Image Encoder to serve as the visual feature for the GOP, resulting in a vector of dimension $d_V = 1408$. Simultaneously, we use BLIP-2 to generate a short descriptive caption for this I-frame. To capture high-level semantic abstractions, this generated caption is then fed into the **all-RoBERTa-large-v1** model from the Sentence Transformers `<cite>` library, yielding a sentence embedding vector (semantic feature) of dimension $d_S = 1024$.
*   **Motion Features:** To capture the spatio-temporal dynamics within each GOP, we utilize the **MViTv2-small** model provided by the Torchvision `<cite>` library. For each GOP, we uniformly sample 16 frames to form the input sequence. Since our objective is representation extraction rather than action recognition, we discard the final classification head and extract the output from the last normalization layer, yielding a motion feature vector of dimension $d_M = 768$.

To mitigate the heavy computational burden of real-time feature extraction, we pre-compute all multimodal features and store them in HDF5 format. During the training and testing phases, the model directly loads these pre-extracted features, which significantly accelerates the overall experimental process.

**Vocabulary and Text Preprocessing.** To construct the vocabulary, we first convert all ground-truth captions to lowercase and remove punctuation. We then filter out rare words that appear fewer than 3 times in the training set to reduce noise. Special tokens, including $\langle \text{S} \rangle$ (used to mark both the start and the end of a sentence), $\langle \text{PAD} \rangle$ (padding), and $\langle \text{UNK} \rangle$ (unknown token), are added to construct the final vocabulary. To ensure consistent sequence modeling, the maximum length of generated captions is set to 20 words for all three datasets.

**Model Architecture.** Our proposed BiDecT model is implemented using the PyTorch framework `<cite-PyTorch2>`. For the Transformer network, we set the model dimension $d_{model}$ to 512, the feed-forward network hidden dimension $d_{ff}$ to 2048, and the number of attention heads to 4. Both the Backward Decoder (BD) and Forward Decoder (FD) are composed of 3 stacked Transformer layers. Following our architectural design, we strictly adopt the Peri-LN strategy to maintain steady gradient flow and balanced variance growth.

**Training Setup.** The model is trained for 16 epochs with a batch size of 64 across all three datasets. We optimize the network parameters using the Adam optimizer with an initial learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, $\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.999$, and a weight decay of $0.5 \times 10^{-5}$. To stabilize the early stages of training, we apply a linear learning rate warmup strategy during the first 3 epochs. The hyperparameter $\lambda$, which balances the loss preferences between the backward and forward decoders, is empirically set to 0.6. Furthermore, we employ a dropout rate of 0.1 and a label smoothing ratio of 0.15 as regularization techniques to prevent overfitting and improve generalization. The best model parameters are ultimately selected based on the overall performance on the validation set.

**Inference and Hardware.** During the inference phase, we employ a beam search strategy with a beam size of 4 for both decoders to generate high-quality captions. All experiments are conducted on NVIDIA A100 GPUs.
