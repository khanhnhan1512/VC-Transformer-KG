## 4.7. Computational Complexity Analysis

To theoretically validate the efficiency of our encoder-free design, we analyze the computational time complexity of the proposed BiDecT architecture in comparison to standard encoder-decoder models.

For a given video represented by $G$ GOPs, the multimodal feature extraction pipeline uniformly produces three distinct feature vectors (appearance, semantic, and motion) per GOP. The multimodal feature embedding module then projects and interleaves them into a unified multimodal sequence of length $V = 3G$. Let $L$ and $L'$ denote the maximum sequence lengths of the forward and backward generated captions, respectively, and let $d$ be the hidden model dimension.

Since feature extraction is typically performed offline prior to training, we restrict our complexity analysis strictly to the core trainable network components. Under this scope, the multimodal feature embedding module contributes only a negligible $\mathcal{O}(V \cdot d)$ cost, as it consists entirely of linear-time projections. 

**Standard Encoder-Decoder Complexity.** In conventional Transformer-based video captioning architectures, multimodal inputs are heavily processed by dedicated Transformer encoders. Within each encoder layer, the self-attention mechanism must compute pairwise affinities across the $G$ tokens of its respective modality, while the subsequent FFN sub-layer processes each token independently. This operation yields a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d + G \cdot d^2)$. Even when multiple encoders operate in parallel across different modalities, the aggregate encoder cost remains bounded by $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d + G \cdot d^2)$ since the number of modalities is a fixed constant. Crucially, as the video duration increases, this quadratic cost $\mathcal{O}(G^2)$ grows exponentially, inevitably becoming the dominant computational bottleneck of the entire system.

**BiDecT Complexity.** By completely discarding the intermediate encoding stage, the BiDecT architecture elegantly circumvents this quadratic bottleneck. Instead, it relies exclusively on the cross-attention mechanisms within the decoders to query the unified multimodal sequence. 
*   For the **Backward Decoder (BD)**, the computational cost per layer arises from the masked self-attention over the reverse text, the cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, and the FFN sub-layer. This yields a per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}({L'}^2 \cdot d + L' \cdot V \cdot d + L' \cdot d^2)$.
*   Similarly, the **Forward Decoder (FD)** computes masked self-attention over the forward text, cross-attention to the multimodal sequence, and the FFN sub-layer. Notably, the FD contains an additional cross-attention sub-layer attending to the global backward context $\overleftarrow{H}$ (which possesses a sequence length of $L'$). This supplementary attention step costs $\mathcal{O}(L \cdot L' \cdot d)$, yielding a total per-layer complexity of $\mathcal{O}(L^2 \cdot d + L \cdot V \cdot d + L \cdot L' \cdot d + L \cdot d^2)$ for the FD.

**Conclusion.** Since the number of decoder layers is a fixed hyperparameter, the overall complexity of the BiDecT architecture is strictly determined by the sum of these two per-layer costs. By removing the encoder, our design completely eliminates the $\mathcal{O}(G^2 \cdot d)$ computational burden. Instead, the model’s direct interaction with the multimodal sequence scales linearly with respect to the video length, bounded by $\mathcal{O}\big((L+L') \cdot V \cdot d \big)$. Furthermore, the remaining computational terms—including decoder self-attention, backward context cross-attention $\mathcal{O}(L \cdot L' \cdot d)$, and FFNs—are all asymptotically constant with respect to the video duration, since $L$, $L'$, and $d$ are strictly bounded by design.

In summary, BiDecT successfully reduces the dominant computational cost associated with video length from quadratic to linear. This paradigm shift offers a highly efficient and scalable architecture for video captioning, proving particularly advantageous when processing long video sequences.
