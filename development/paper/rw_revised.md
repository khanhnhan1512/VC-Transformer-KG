# 2. Related Work

## 2.1. Multimodal Feature Extraction and Intermediate Processing

Transformer-based video captioning methods generally adopt one of two feature extraction paradigms: offline feature extraction, where features are pre-computed and used as fixed model inputs [Bidirectional transformer with knowledge graph for video captioning | Concept-Aware Video Captioning: Describing Videos With Effective Prior Information](), and end-to-end video Transformers, where the model jointly learns visual representations and caption generation from raw video inputs, as exemplified by SwinBERT [SwinBERT: End-to-End Transformers with Sparse Attention for Video Captioning]().

In practice, extracted features are rarely passed directly to the decoder. A recurring motivation for additional processing is that extractor outputs are typically holistic and frame-level, lacking the explicit object-level and relational structure required for fine-grained caption generation. Methods therefore differ primarily in the type of missing structure they attempt to recover. IVRC [Rethink video retrieval representation for video captioning]() introduces a token-shift module to strengthen temporal modeling and a Refineformer for cross-modal spatial alignment. Track4Cap [Frame-by-Frame Multi-Object Tracking-Guided Video Captioning]() employs a multi-object tracking module to preserve object identity across frames, and an object-relation encoder to model inter-object relationships. KG-VCN [Fully exploring object relation interaction and hidden state attention for video captioning]() models inter-object relations by employing a Graph Transformer alongside an external knowledge graph, while HSRA [Action-Driven Semantic Representation and Aggregation for Video Captioning]() structures visual semantics into an object–action–event hierarchy, employing a DETR-inspired detection layer to make actions and events explicit. These modules can yield measurable gains in their targeted settings; however, they share a common trade-off: each additional component deepens the processing pipeline, increases structural and computational overhead, and makes caption quality dependent on intermediate predictors such as detectors, trackers, or graph builders, whose errors may propagate downstream.

This trade-off becomes increasingly difficult to justify as pre-trained extractors continue to improve. Modern large-scale pre-trained models such as CLIP [Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision]() and MViTv2 [MViTv2: Improved Multiscale Vision Transformers for Classification and Detection]() already produce semantically rich and well-structured representations. Given these advances, additional intermediate encoding stages risk introducing redundant transformation and noise propagation rather than meaningful representational gain. These observations raise the possibility that modern pre-trained representations may already be sufficiently informative for more direct multimodal integration strategies with reduced intermediate processing.

## 2.2. Semantic Knowledge Augmentation for Video Captioning

Beyond structural processing, another major challenge is that visual features alone often provide limited high-level semantic information and weak coverage of rare or long-tailed vocabulary. A substantial body of work addresses this limitation through external semantic knowledge injection, which can be broadly organized into three strategies reflecting an evolving understanding of the underlying problem.

The first strategy retrieves static knowledge from external knowledge bases. TextKG [Text with Knowledge Graph Augmented Transformer for Video Captioning]() retrieves commonsense facts from ConceptNet and dataset-specific knowledge from speech transcripts, integrating them via a two-stream framework, while BTKG [Bidirectional transformer with knowledge graph for video captioning]() predicts inter-object relationships using TransE [TransE]() and integrates the resulting relational structure into the encoder. Although these approaches provide semantic information beyond what visual features alone can convey, the retrieved knowledge is often only loosely conditioned on the specific video content, making relevance control a persistent challenge.

The second strategy pursues scene-adaptive knowledge construction to address this relevance problem. MK-VC [Scene adaptive dynamic multi-modal knowledge for video captioning]() combines static commonsense with dynamically retrieved video-related knowledge, EMKG [Towards generalized video captioning: An effective multi-modal knowledge graph perspective]() constructs a ConceptVision Knowledge Graph that couples detected object features with commonsense relations, and DSSM-KG [DSSM-KG: Dual-Stream State-Space Modeling with Adaptive Knowledge Injection for Video Captioning]() pairs a Mamba-Transformer hybrid for spatiotemporal modeling with adaptive knowledge injection into the decoder. While these methods improve relevance over static retrieval, they do so through more elaborate processing pipelines requiring additional stages for concept extraction, knowledge construction, and knowledge fusion. Notably, MK-VC and EMKG explicitly introduce adaptive suppression mechanisms partly intended to filter noisy or irrelevant knowledge, suggesting that pipeline complexity has itself become a source of the problem it was designed to solve.

The third strategy bypasses explicit knowledge construction altogether by deriving semantic representations directly from large-scale vision-language models (VLMs). Zhang et al. [Pretrained Image-Text Models are Secretly Video Captioners]() demonstrate that an image captioning model built on BLIP-2 [BLIP-2: Bootstrapping Language-Image Pre-training with Frozen Image Encoders and Large Language Models](), post-trained on only a small set of video-text pairs, can rival specialized video captioning systems without relying on complex video-specific architectures, requiring only simple frame concatenation. This finding suggests that the semantic knowledge embedded in large-scale VLMs transfers effectively to the video domain, offering semantically grounded representations without the structural overhead of explicit knowledge graph pipelines.
